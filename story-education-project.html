Education Project is Empowering People with T1D in their Managing of Diabetes

Nicole Lopez
August 23, 2020
5
min read
Remote

As COVID cases began rising, so did the calls to Dexcom for customer support.

“Our role as educators was already heavily impacted. People want to understand the benefits of CGM before using it. Especially if you’re newly diagnosed, this is all new to you, and people are usually resistant to wearing medical equipment on their body. I completely get that.” Tiana explains that many patients who were previously helped by their healthcare providers to control and manage their data, could now only resort to virtual appointments. But Dexcom’s older patient demographic is large, and providing support so their experience with diabetes is manageable is essential.

“It’s important to help people understand the benefits of this [technology]. That it is not for your doctor only, but for you to self-manage.”
Tiana, T1D Participant

‍

The Diabetes Design Initiative’s latest project aims to do just that. With the help of experts on the field and the diabetes community, four design students are creating a simpler way for people to understand, visualize, and manage diabetes, focusing on interactive visualizations as a learning tool.


Over the past eight weeks, our designers have met remotely with healthcare providers and CGM users to envision a tool that will help people understand and manage insulin timing and dosing.

Currently, providers spend a considerably reducible amount of time explaining this challenging and hard-to-visualize information to their patients. With the enthusiasm and support from experts in the field like Gayle Lorenzi and Arizona Milotich, our design team has been able to prioritize users and design prototypes that anticipate their needs.  

‍

It is really satisfying to have my feedback be directly used in research.


Tiana talks about her latest involvement in this project as a participant. “I’ve always wanted to be more involved in some capacity in using my experience to actually advance the field. It is really satisfying to have my feedback be directly used in research. Not just to help educate patients, which is what I currently do and love, but also in making the technology become more advanced - [to have] the actual agency to improve.”  Tiana admits her experience with DDI has sparked a passion for more involvement in technology development.
